INTERIOR

The primary purpose of your building's interior elements is to serve the needs and space requirements of its occupants. Defining features include walls, ceilings, floors, doors, windows, and storage needs. Also, the heating, cooling, ventilation, plumbing, and electrical systems are arranged to meet the needs of each room and space.

 

INSPECTION PROCESS

As documented by this report, the focus of the building inspection is on the functional rather than the appearance aspects of your building's interior elements. The investigation of the internal elements includes the examination of walls ceilings and floors; steps, stairways, and railings; balconies; countertops and a representative number of installed cabinets, and a representative number of doors and windows. This inspection does not typically include an examination of surface finishes such as paint, wallpaper, or other forms of finish treatment, or installed elements such as carpeting, window treatments, central vacuums, household appliances, and recreational facilities (pools, spas, etc.).

 

The primary mode of examination of interior elements is visual; surfaces, coverings, and obstructions are not disturbed in the course of the examination. If observed, the inspector will report signs of abnormal or harmful water penetration into the building or signs of abnormal or harmful condensation on building components. The inspection is not a guarantee all areas may be discovered during a single visit home inspection and we recommended having your home inspected at least every other year. This examination does not typically include assessment for air quality, moisture problems that may result in visible or concealed mold growth, the presence of toxic or hazardous materials, the presence of radon gas, and contaminants either present from construction or past use of the property.

INTERIOR SURFACE FINISHES: Interior finishes provide for the decorative surfaces of walls and ceilings, are most commonly applied as drywall; other common materials include paneling, tile, wood, and plaster. Ceilings may also have a textured finish, usually trowelled or sprayed on. These surfaces should get monitored for change over time: minor cracks may appear, usually at edges and corners, which are often due to normal construction material shrinkage. However, cracks of irregular shape and greater than hairline in size may be an indication of structural change and should get investigated.
 
FLOOR FINISHES: Floors provide a durable surface for foot traffic, and are usually a combination of materials that may include carpeting, hardwood flooring, laminate flooring, stone or ceramic tile, or resilient flooring. Hardwood and wood laminate floors are susceptible to change due to humidity, and efforts to control humidity, as either wood shrinkage or swelling can have both cosmetic and functional impacts.
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
CABINETS AND COUNTERTOPS: Cabinets provide space for storage while countertops provide working surfaces, usually located in the kitchen, laundry, and bathroom areas of the home. The appearance of water stains in Bathrooms and Kitchen cabinets is a very common issue and can easily not get reported on during inspection due to personal items in these locations. Personal items are not moved during an inspection. Water-damaged interior cabinet bottoms are also not reported on unless it is new construction.
 
STAIRS: Stairs provide the means of access to different levels of the home. Safe passage is reliant on sound design and construction practices, with provision for handrails and guards to reduce the risk of fall injuries. Although the need for repairs is minimal, stairs and railings should be monitored and maintained with safety as the primary consideration.
 
 

Cosmetic damage will not be reported during this inspection. The purchaser should make his or her observations of cosmetic damage and any required repairs. Areas blocked by personal property, including but not limited to the wall and floor surfaces, cannot be inspected and are excluded from this report.

 

DOORS: The primary purpose of interior doors is to meet the privacy needs of various rooms and areas of the home, or in the case of closets, to enclose storage areas. Wood is the most common material used indoor fabrication, although door styles include glass and mirror panes or panels. Door fit and operation, as well as hardware, may be subject to adjustment and maintenance. Note: Exterior Glass doors without tempered glass may be considered a potential safety hazard.

 
WINDOWS: The primary purpose of windows is to provide light and ventilation to the home. Typical window sash and frame materials include vinyl, metal, and wood. Maintenance needs vary with the type and style of windows; generally, windows should get periodically checked for operation, weather tightness, and deterioration. As well, sealed panes should be monitored for loss of seal, and may require repair or replacement as needed.  Consider an Examination for the presence of condensation on windows during cold weather. Often the cause of condensation or ice on windows is due to high humidity levels in the structure, in which case efforts to control humidity should be investigated and implemented. Improving airflow at windows may also help to reduce the occurrence of condensation, such as opening blinds or curtains, and ensuring air from forced-air registers is directed towards the windows. Note: Exterior Glass windows without tempered glass may be considered a potential safety hazard when the risk of falling is present.
 
FIREPLACES AND STOVES: Fireplaces and stoves are generally installed in homes for aesthetic reasons and ambiance, although these may also serve to provide heating. Proper design, installation, and maintenance are essential. Wood stoves and fireplaces require particular attention, due to the build-up of creosote in chimneys, which can become a fire hazard. Care in the use and maintenance of fuel-burning appliances should be regarded as the primary considerations for safety.
 
At the time of inspection, smoke alarms are not tested. Smoke alarm technology is evolving and current technology is going in the direction of recommending that only Photoelectric smoke alarms be installed in the home due to the nuisance tripping and other human factors involved with misuse and maintenance associated with Ionization type smoke alarms. It is not possible in the context of the home inspection to determine why types of alarms are installed in the home. You are encouraged to install and maintain any type of alarm in the home and you are encouraged to upgrade alarms to photoelectric type alarms. Contact your Local Fire Department for an inspection of your home's fire safety systems.
At the time of inspection Carbon Monoxide alarm/detectors are not tested. I recommend that prior to moveâu0080u0090in, that all Carbon Monoxide alarms/detectors be tested and have their batteries replaced. It is recommended that Carbon Monoxide detectors that are older than 5 years should be replaced by a licensed electrical contractor if they are hardâu0080u0090wired, and replaced by the homeowner/handyperson if they are battery operated. These devices are currently required, according to Washington State Law to be maintained by the tenant/homeowner according to the manufacturer's recommendations/instructions. For optimum safety, Carbon Monoxide alarm/detectors are required in the immediate vicinity of bedrooms and on each floor level of the home. Alarm/detectors must be maintained free of dust and debris which can interfere with operation. Please be aware that residential Carbon Monoxide detectors are cumulative and are designed to not sound with low levels of carbon monoxide. Some people are more susceptible than others to low levels of carbon monoxide and I consider it prudent to familiarize yourself with the symptoms/warning signs of Carbon Monoxide. Detectors that meet the ULâu0080u00902034 requirements for detectors installed in residential construction are not allowed to sound at continuous CO levels up to 30 ppm, 70 ppm for 4 hours, 150 ppm for up to 50 minutes, and 400 ppm for up to 15 minutes.
 
APPLIANCES: We may test kitchen appliances for basic functionality but cannot evaluate them for their performance nor the variety of their settings or cycles.  Appliances older than ten years may exhibit decreased efficiency.  All appliances should have a replacement budget in places, such as free-standing appliances, refrigerators, freezers, ice makers, trash compactors, microwaves, built-in toasters, coffee-makers, can-openers, blenders, instant hot-water dispensers, water-purifiers, barbecues, grills, or rotisseries, timers, clocks, thermostats, the self-cleaning and cooking capability of ovens, and concealed or countertop lighting, which is convenient but often installed after the initial construction and not wired to national electrical standards. 
 
 
Note: Icemakers, Insta-Hot water dispensers, and refrigerator water dispensers are only evaluated for leaks around fixtures only. These features are known to fail and not operate frequently. If concerned with these fixtures buyer should request the owner verify the operation of appliances and fixtures. 
These items should be considered outside the scope of the inspection. Microwaves are tested for ventilation and are not examined to determine how well they heat food. Home appliances are also not moved during the home inspection. Portable dishwashers are also not inspected, as they require a connection to facilitate testing. **Concerned buyers are recommended to purchase a Home Warranty from a third-party service to cover major appliances (Furnace, Water Heater, Stove, Fridge, etc.).

Be aware that additional defects may be discovered during a more comprehensive evaluation by an appropriate professional. 
 
We inspect a representative number of windows and doors, switches, and outlets. We do not evaluate window treatments, move furnishings or possessions, lift carpets or rugs, empty closets or cabinets. We may not comment on cracks that appear around windows and doors, along with the lines of framing members or along seams of drywall and plasterboard.  Caused by minor movements, such as wood shrinkage, natural settling, and seismic activity, and will often reappear if not correctly repaired.  Such cracks can become the subject of disputes, and are therefore best evaluated by a specialist. We do not verify whether all walls, floors, ceilings, doorways, cabinets, and window openings are square, straight, level or plumb. We do not report on cosmetic conditions related to the condition of interior components. Note: Determining the condition of the waterproofing on interior below-grade walls is outside the scope of this inspection. Testing, identifying, or identifying the source of environmental pollutants or odors (including but not limited to lead, mold, allergens, odors from household pets, and cigarette smoke) is beyond the scope of our service, but can become equally contentious or challenging to eradicate. The conditions inside walls, behind occupant’s stuff, behind wallpaper or paneling, under flooring, and areas not visible cannot be judged and are not inspected. Do a careful check during the final walkthrough. 

Identification, investigation, or recommendations regarding MOLD and/or the presence of MOLD are outside the scope of this inspection. Any comments made are as a courtesy and may not address all areas. If concerned have tested by a mold inspector.   We recommend you carefully determine and schedule whatever remedial services may be deemed advisable or necessary before the close of escrow.

INTERIOR ELEMENTS ASSESSMENT SUMMARY:
 
 
 
 

INTERIOR

INTERIOR

The primary purpose of your building's interior elements is to serve the needs and space requirements of its occupants. Defining features include walls, ceilings, floors, doors, windows, and storage needs. Also, the heating, cooling, ventilation, plumbing, and electrical systems are arranged to meet the needs of each room and space.

 

INSPECTION PROCESS

As documented by this report, the focus of the building inspection is on the functional rather than the appearance aspects of your building's interior elements. The investigation of the internal elements includes the examination of walls ceilings and floors; steps, stairways, and railings; balconies; countertops and a representative number of installed cabinets, and a representative number of doors and windows. This inspection does not typically include an examination of surface finishes such as paint, wallpaper, or other forms of finish treatment, or installed elements such as carpeting, window treatments, central vacuums, household appliances, and recreational facilities (pools, spas, etc.).

 

The primary mode of examination of interior elements is visual; surfaces, coverings, and obstructions are not disturbed in the course of the examination. If observed, the inspector will report signs of abnormal or harmful water penetration into the building or signs of abnormal or harmful condensation on building components. The inspection is not a guarantee all areas may be discovered during a single visit home inspection and we recommended having your home inspected at least every other year. This examination does not typically include assessment for air quality, moisture problems that may result in visible or concealed mold growth, the presence of toxic or hazardous materials, the presence of radon gas, and contaminants either present from construction or past use of the property.

INTERIOR SURFACE FINISHES: Interior finishes provide for the decorative surfaces of walls and ceilings, are most commonly applied as drywall; other common materials include paneling, tile, wood, and plaster. Ceilings may also have a textured finish, usually trowelled or sprayed on. These surfaces should get monitored for change over time: minor cracks may appear, usually at edges and corners, which are often due to normal construction material shrinkage. However, cracks of irregular shape and greater than hairline in size may be an indication of structural change and should get investigated.
 
FLOOR FINISHES: Floors provide a durable surface for foot traffic, and are usually a combination of materials that may include carpeting, hardwood flooring, laminate flooring, stone or ceramic tile, or resilient flooring. Hardwood and wood laminate floors are susceptible to change due to humidity, and efforts to control humidity, as either wood shrinkage or swelling can have both cosmetic and functional impacts.

WINDOWS: The primary purpose of windows is to provide light and ventilation to the home. Typical window sash and frame materials include vinyl, metal, and wood. Note: Exterior Glass windows without tempered glass may be considered a potential safety hazard when the risk of falling is present.

Care in the use and maintenance of fuel-burning appliances should be regarded as the primary considerations for safety.

We inspect a representative number of windows and doors, switches, and outlets. We do not evaluate window treatments, move furnishings or possessions, lift carpets or rugs, empty closets or cabinets. We may not comment on cracks that appear around windows and doors, along with the lines of framing members or along seams of drywall and plasterboard.  Caused by minor movements, such as wood shrinkage, natural settling, and seismic activity, and will often reappear if not correctly repaired.  Such cracks can become the subject of disputes, and are therefore best evaluated by a specialist. We do not verify whether all walls, floors, ceilings, doorways, cabinets, and window openings are square, straight, level or plumb. We do not report on cosmetic conditions related to the condition of interior components. Note: Determining the condition of the waterproofing on interior below-grade walls is outside the scope of this inspection. Interior walls are visually inspected for stains and damage inaccessible areas that are not blocked or covered by occupant’s belongings. 
Floor covering such as carpeting and rugs do not get lifted. Hidden damage or stains may exist before close buyers should verify with owner floor conditions.  Determining the effectiveness of insulated glass windows is not always possible due to temperature, weather, and lighting conditions.  Check with owners for further information.   All fireplaces should be cleaned and inspected on a regular basis to make sure that no cracks have developed.  Large fires in the firebox can overheat the firebox and flue liners, sometimes resulting in internal damage. Testing, identifying, or identifying the source of environmental pollutants or odors (including but not limited to lead, mold, allergens, odors from household pets, and cigarette smoke) is beyond the scope of our service, but can become equally contentious or challenging to eradicate. The conditions inside walls, behind occupant’s stuff, behind wallpaper or paneling, under flooring, and areas not visible cannot be judged and are not inspected. Do a careful check during the final walkthrough.
